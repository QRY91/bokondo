The Professional Journey

Psychology studies, game design, callcenter work, bootcamp, startup burnout, back to school at 33. Not the typical CS path. But I've learned that caring about the craft—really caring about building things that work—matters more than the route you took to get here.

Phase 1: Thrown In Deep (2020)

Months out of a C# bootcamp, I was solo dev on enterprise logistics for Maersk. API integrations with German customs systems, client relationships, project management—scope that usually requires a team. Learned fast because I had to.

Phase 2: Learning Through Friction (2021-2022)

Worked in environments where thoroughness was discouraged but quality was still expected. The classic double-bind. Instead of just moving on, I documented what worked and what didn't—patterns that now inform how I build things.

Phase 3: Breadth and Depth (2023-2024)

Explored widely: cybersecurity fundamentals, game design, hardware configuration, Go, Rust, Python. Built real projects in each area. The goal was understanding how different domains think about problems—patterns that transfer across fields.

Phase 4: Back to School (2024-2025)

Built Panopticron during my BornDigital internship—a monitoring dashboard now running in production. Presented it as my graduate program capstone. Formalized what I'd already been doing in the field.

Why Engineering

I care about the craft. Not just shipping features, but understanding why things work, building things that last, and leaving codebases better than I found them.

The non-traditional path taught me something: difficult experiences sharpen your thinking if you let them. Every frustrating system I've worked with became a lesson in what not to build. That perspective is hard to get from a smooth career trajectory.

I'm looking for work where careful thinking is valued, where there's room to dig into problems properly, and where the goal is building things that actually work well—not just things that ship.
